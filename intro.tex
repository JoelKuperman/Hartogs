\section{Introduction}
Well foundedness undoubtedly plays a decisive role in all of Set Theory. This
paper intends to be a contribution to the question of how this notion persists
when considering restricted sets of axioms that conform to the “working
mathematician's” intuition. At the same time, we are interested in examples of
elementary (or easy-to-state) problems, solvable by the use of set-theoretic
axioms that do not defy any conservative intuition, but require notions beyond
naïve concepts.

Take, for example, the notion of cardinality. It is equivalent to $\AC$ over
$\ZF$ that cardinalities are well \emph{ordered}; notably, it is an open
question whether the fact that the universe of sets is well founded under the
relation
\begin{quote}
  $A \leq_c B$ if and only if there is an injection from $A$ to $B$,
\end{quote}
implies $\AC$.

Nevertheless, we consider the Axiom of Choice to lie beyond our list of
“intuitive” axioms, which will also exclude Foundation. In this restricted
setting, it is clear that we cannot expect well foundedness of cardinalities but
we still have that cardinalities cannot decrease at an iterated-exponential rate
(much in the same way that it is impossible to iterate $\log_2$ on the real
numbers indefinitely). Namely, $\ZF$ proves \cite[Exr.~I(12)]{kunen1980} that
\begin{theorem}\label{informal_hartogs_set}
  There is no sequence $\{X_n\}_{n\in\omega}$ such that  for all $n$, there is an injection
    from $\mathcal{P}(X_{n+1})$ to $X_n$.
\end{theorem}

In this article we restrict ourselves a little more and prove the above fact without using the Replacement axiom, motivated by the discussion in \cite{PedroStack}.

%Taking into consideration that ZF proves there cannot be a sequence $\{X_n\}_{n\in\omega}$ such that for each $n\in\omega$ the inequality $\mathcal{P}(X_{n+1}) <_c X_n$ holds, we will show a proof of this result in a weaker subtheory of ZF.

Moreover, we show that not only can this sequence not exist as a set, it cannot even be defined in the metatheory.
More precisely, it is inconsistent with Zermelo set theory to have a formula $\Phi(\cdot,\cdot)$ such that for each $n\in\omega$ there are $X$ and $Y$ with
\[
	\Phi(n,X) \land \Phi(n+1,Y) \land \mathcal{P}(Y) \leq_c X.
\]
Finally, we prove that the theorem fails if we allow infinitely many
definitions, one for each term of the sequence. That is, for
each $n$ there is a formula $\Phi_n$ that defines a set and it is
consistent with $\zfc$ that for every $n$,
\[
	\Phi_n (X) \land \Phi_{n+1} (Y) \land \mathcal{P}(Y) \leq_c X.
\]

The proof of the last theorem required us to extend a countable model of $\zfc$ to a pointwise definable one, which \cite{hamkins} shows can always be done.
